Our history

From global partnership to Haiti's kitchens

PPAF began with ambassadors and a United Nations idea: governments, businesses, and nonprofits can accomplish more when they work together. Over time, that idea became practical work across three countries.

01
The beginning

An alliance born at the United Nations

The foundation grew from the vision of ambassadors from Madagascar, the Dominican Republic, and Haiti. They believed companies—large and small—could work with governments and nonprofits to create meaningful change in vulnerable communities.

The UN Economic and Social Council endorsed the concept and established the UN Public-Private Alliance for Rural Development. That initiative later evolved into the US-based 501(c)(3) Public-Private Alliance Foundation.

02
Madagascar

Turning rural development ideas into a plan

PPAF's earliest country work was in Madagascar. Government leaders, the Food and Agriculture Organization, businesses, and local partners explored rural development, microfinance, handicraft exports, and a business-oriented national development plan.

03
Dominican Republic

From remittances to renewable energy

In the Dominican Republic, PPAF worked on remittances as a development tool, technical support for small fish farmers, agricultural export opportunities, and renewable-energy seminars held both in Santo Domingo and at UN Headquarters.

04
Haiti after 2010

A crisis made the daily act of cooking impossible to ignore

After Haiti's devastating 2010 earthquake, PPAF focused on a less-visible emergency: how families cook. Reliance on charcoal and wood damages lungs, consumes household income, and accelerates environmental loss—especially affecting women and children.

PPAF and its partners first explored ethanol stoves and locally produced fuel. The work broadened to biodigesters and solar cooking, including Haiti's first college-level course on the subject through Université Notre Dame d'Haiti.

05
Local resilience

Hinche and Jacmel carry the work forward

Disrupted supply chains made local ingenuity even more important. Colleagues in Hinche developed locally made solar cookers and operate a workshop for training, demonstrations, and sales. In Jacmel, colleagues continue outreach and build a roster of future customers.

Cookers are sold far below cost to make them accessible and to nurture local entrepreneurial skills.

06
The thread through it all

Turning policy into practice

Across Madagascar, the Dominican Republic, and Haiti, PPAF has moved between global institutions and local realities. Its history is a long experiment in partnership: share knowledge widely, listen locally, and turn ambitious development goals into tools people can use.
